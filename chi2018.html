Background

Embodied Interaction

  • Embodied cognition: providing the link between perception and action
  • Tools for solving spatial problems can be cognitively grasped, understood, and simulated into one’s body schema
  • Feeling, action, and thought are functionally integral to cognition
  • Divide between natural thought processes and the virtual confinement of traditional computer interaction (2D)

  • Background

    Graphical user interfaces (GUIs):

  • Inflexible in use, and inadequate for users to perceive and process information
  • Limit ways geospatial data can be represented

  • GRASS GIS Command Line Interface (CLI) & Graphical User Interface (GUI)

    • Background

    Tangible user interfaces (TUIs):

  • Bridge theoretical divide seen with GUIs
  • Offer more natural and intuitive mode of interaction
  • Allow users to cognitively grasp and physically manipulate 3D data
  • Spport more effective and natural learning

    • Background

    Tangibles in Education

  • Spatially-focused curricula = improved student success
  • Still difficult for students to visualize spatial relations
  • Solution? Use tangibles in the classroom.
  • TUIs allow students with little to no computer experience interactively explore, model, visualize, and think about complex, spatial scientific problems

    • Background

    Typical Teaching Methods for Terrain Analysis:

    in-situ surveying, drawing contour maps, and building physical models.

    Only some directly teach students how to translate between 2D and 3D space. Tangibles can allow students to associate 2D field, map, and GIS data simultaneously with complex, 3D landscape structures and therefore reduce the cognitive burden of computing what is logically implied by 2D spatial information.

    Spatial tasks can be performed computationally to efficiently store, model, and analyze large sets of spatial data and solve complex spatiotemporal problems.

    • Computer-aided design (CAD) and 3D modeling software are used to interactively, computationally model, analyze, and animate complex 3D forms
    • Geographic information systems (GIS), geospatial programming, and spatial statistics are used to mathematically model, simulate, and optimize multidimensional spatial patterns and processes
